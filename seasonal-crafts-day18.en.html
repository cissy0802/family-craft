Make Together: Seasonal Crafts

2026-07-03 · Day 18
This one barely costs a thing — take a short walk and you've got your materials. It's high summer now, and the sidewalk offers up wide leaves, fallen petals, a few pebbles, a stray twig. Spread them on the table and you've got a whole afternoon. "Seasonal crafts" isn't about making something polished — it's about taking the abstract idea of "what season is it out there?" and turning it into something your kids can touch, glue down, and hang up. Four things: a nature collage, a seasonal mobile, painted stone friends, and a "season jar" you can play with all year. Go out and collect, come home and make, then write the date on it — dig it out this time next year and it's the best growth record you'll have.

1. Nature Collage: Turn Your Finds into a Picture把捡来的做成一幅画

⏱ 30–40 minMaterials: leaves & petals + glue + card

Prep & Safety

Materials: a handful of leaves, petals, grass, fluffy seeds (like dandelions) collected outdoors; a stiff sheet of paper or a piece cut from an old cardboard box; a glue stick or white glue; optionally a pen for a background. How to play: first arrange the pieces on the paper into something — a round leaf for a body, two pointy leaves for ears makes a little fox; a row of petals becomes a sun; scattered bits become a meadow. Lay it out, get it looking right, then glue it down one piece at a time. Press it under a few books; it sets in about half an hour. Don't aim for realism — whatever it turns into is what it is.

Safety: shake off what you collect, and skip anything with bugs or mold; never pick unknown berries, mushrooms, or plants with milky sap or thorns. Watch the 3-year-old for choking — small seeds and berries in the mouth are a real risk; keep eyes on her the whole time and agree up front: "hands only, nothing in the mouth." Scissors and white glue are for the grown-up or 9-year-old; some people react to petals, so wash hands after.
Roles: cutting the card and squeezing the glue go to the grown-up or 9-year-old; the 3-year-old arranges and presses.

Age 3

The collecting is her favorite part — crouching down picking pieces, "This red one! This big one!" keeps her happy for ages. At home she "arranges" and "presses": you dab the glue, she lays a leaf on and pats it flat with her palm. Never mind if it's crooked — every piece she sticks on counts. Give her her own sheet to fill; filled up is a finished work.

Age 9

She can "design" a whole themed picture: a tree through the seasons, an underwater scene, her own face. Challenge her to use only the natural colors of the materials, no pen — "mixing" shades from the different greens of leaves and the reds and yellows of petals. Go further: press leaves inside a thick book for a couple of days to make flat "pressed flowers," then collage with those — they last a long time. Ask: why do leaves turn brittle and change color when they dry? (The water leaves, and the green chlorophyll slowly breaks down, so the yellows and reds hiding underneath show through — same reason autumn leaves change.)

Sisters Together

Make a "big collaboration picture": the older sister handles composition and the main lines (the branches of a big tree, the horizon between sky and ground), the younger one "fills it in" — sticking leaves on the tree, scattering petals and grass for a garden. Big sister is the designer setting the frame, little sister is the "colorist" filling it up (no swapping jobs: layout is big sister's, filling is little sister's). One sheet, two kids, done when it's full.

The Why + a Twist

A collage plays with "one thing can stand for another" — a leaf isn't a leaf anymore, it's an ear, a roof, a fish scale. That "this-for-that" imagination is the bedrock of drawing and storytelling. Twist: photograph the finished picture as a postcard; or make a "bookmark" — press a small leaf inside clear tape, laminate it into a thin strip, and it lasts a whole year.

2. Seasonal Mobile: Hang the Season Up把季节挂起来

⏱ 25–35 minMaterials: twig + string + nature bits

Prep & Safety

Materials: a chopstick-length dry twig (the crossbar); a few lengths of cotton or old yarn; summer nature bits — dried flowers, big leaves, shells, small pinecones, little berries strung together. How to play: hold the twig horizontal, tie a string to each end and hang it (from a doorframe, curtain rod, chair back — anything); then drop several strings of different lengths from the crossbar, tying a nature bit to the bottom of each. Give it a tap and the whole thing sways — a "summer mobile." The key is staggering the lengths — don't make them all level, or it won't look good swaying.

Safety: choose a twig that's dry, not shedding bark, and not sharp; wrap or snap off any sharp end. Keep the strings short — the hanging strings should be too short to reach a child's neck, and hang the mobile up high where the kids can't reach it, to prevent tangling. The 3-year-old doesn't touch long strings — she just picks bits and hands them over. Shells and small berries are tiny; watch her for choking throughout.
Roles: tying, knotting, and deciding where to hang it go to the grown-up or 9-year-old; the 3-year-old picks the decorations and calls out "hang this one here."

Age 3

She's the "judge of beauty": she picks the bits she likes from the pile and hands them to you, "Hang this! Hang that!" Once it's up, let her reach out and give it a gentle nudge and watch the whole thing sway — that "I touched it and it moved" cause-and-effect is something she'll do again and again. Use as many of her picks as you can; she'll feel truly part of it.

Age 9

She's the "balance engineer": both sides of the crossbar need equal weight or it tips; load one side heavier and the whole thing tilts. Let her adjust — slide a heavy bit toward the middle, or add one to the other side, until the crossbar hangs level. This is a first lesson in levers and center of gravity. Then challenge her: design a "changing mobile" that swaps in seasonal bits each time the season turns, four changes a year.

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "engineer" tying strings and adjusting balance; little sister is "procurement + QA," handing over bits and, once it's done, being first to nudge it and "sign off" (no swapping jobs: knotting and balancing are big sister's, choosing and checking are little sister's). Together they name the mobile and hang it somewhere everyone passes, giving it a nudge each time.

The Why + a Twist

A mobile hangs level thanks to "center of gravity" — the pull of the weight on each side about the midpoint has to cancel out. Heavier on one side and it sinks; slide it toward the middle and its pull shrinks. Same thing as a seesaw or a balance scale. Twist: skip the twig and use a wire coat hanger as the crossbar; or make it a "wind chime" with little metal pieces, old keys, or shells at the bottom that clink in the breeze.

3. Painted Stone Friends: Give a Rock a Face给石头一张脸

⏱ 30 min + dryingMaterials: stones + paint/markers

Prep & Safety

Materials: a few palm-sized, smooth stones (from a riverbank or park, washed and dried); acrylic paint or waterproof markers (regular watercolor works too, just not waterproof); a fine brush. How to play: first look at what each stone's shape "looks like" — a flat round one is a ladybug, a long one a little fish, a pointy one a house. Work with the shape, don't force it: a round stone with a few black dots and two stripes is a ladybug; add eyes and a mouth and it's instantly a little monster or a cat. Paint, let dry, and you've got a nest of stone friends that "live" on the windowsill.

Safety: wash the stones and skip any that are cracked or too sharp. Choose non-toxic kids' acrylic — it stains clothes and tables, so lay down newspaper, wear old clothes, and don't sweat the mess. Guard the 3-year-old hard against swallowing small stones — give her big ones that absolutely won't fit in her mouth. Keep paint away from eyes; wash hands after. Markers are easier and cleaner than paint, so give the 3-year-old fat markers first.
Roles: the grown-up picks and washes the stones ahead of time so kids go straight to painting. If acrylic worries you, give the 3-year-old markers only.

Age 3

She "just scribbles": give her a big stone and a few fat markers, and whatever it turns into is her masterpiece — a stone full of wild lines counts. To nudge her along, dot two circles on the stone for eyes; she'll see "it's a face!" and add a mouth and hair herself. The point is the discovery that "you can draw on a stone," not making it look like anything.

Age 9

She can make a "themed set": a family of stones (Mom, Dad, two sisters — four stones), a set of animals, a row of emotion faces (happy / angry / surprised). Challenge her to look at the stone's actual shape before deciding what to paint, rather than forcing a round stone into a giraffe. Level up: arrange stones into a "stone story" — a row that tells one thing — or paint a set of "stone game pieces" you can actually play five-in-a-row with.

Sisters Together

Build a "stone town": big sister paints the houses, trees, and roads (the tricky stuff), little sister paints the people and animals who live there (simple round faces). Set the town out on a table or balcony and make up together who lives where (no swapping jobs: buildings are big sister's, residents are little sister's). Every new stone you find later gets a new resident added to the town.

The Why + a Twist

Seeing what a stone "looks like" is the brain playing "find the pattern" — humans naturally spot faces and animals in random shapes (same with clouds, or specks on a wall). Creating from an existing shape is easier to start with than a blank sheet. Twist: hide the painted stones around the neighborhood as "lucky stones" for other kids to find (write a kind word on the back); or make "paperweights" for the desk.

4. Season Jar: Bottle Up Today's Summer装一罐「今天的夏天」

⏱ 20 min · lasts all yearMaterials: empty jar + nature bits + paper

Prep & Safety

Materials: a clean empty glass jar or clear plastic box (an old jam jar is perfect); things collected today that stand for this season — a green leaf, a flower, a pebble, a bit of dried grass, even a note reading "34°C today, so hot"; a pen. How to play: put the bits in the jar and tuck in a note: date + today's weather + one line, "This summer I remember…", put the lid on, set it on the windowsill. This is the first jar of four. Agree to make one each season; collect all four in a year and lined up in a row they are a whole year.

Safety: don't use thin glass that could shatter and cut — with a 3-year-old around, prefer a lidded clear plastic box, set high on the sill out of her reach. Shake nature bits clean and skip fresh fruit that will rot or mold (dried lasts longer). When the 3-year-old drops bits in, steady the rim so she doesn't pinch her fingers.
Roles: the grown-up handles the glass jar, the 3-year-old drops the bits in, the 9-year-old writes the note.

Age 3

She "drops them in": posting leaves, flowers, and pebbles into the jar one by one is as fun as dropping coins. Let her pick "three favorites from today" to go in. It's fine that she can't explain the seasons — you record it for her: "This is little sister's summer at three; she put in one big green leaf." Years from now, that line is more precious than anything.

Age 9

She's the "recorder": she writes the note — date, weather, temperature, what you did today, one thing she'll remember about this summer. Challenge her to be "time manager": mark the next jar's date on the calendar (the day the season turns) and remind the whole family. Ask: why do the four seasons take turns? (Earth is tilted as it orbits the Sun, so at different points the sunlight hits us at different angles — straight-on and hot is summer, slanted and cold is winter — two sentences is plenty.)

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "curator" writing labels, ordering the jars, and deciding where they sit; little sister is the "collector" going out to gather and dropping bits in (no swapping jobs: recording and managing are big sister's, gathering and dropping are little sister's). This is a promise that lasts all year — each time the season turns, the two of them go out for the new season's finds and add a jar together. By next summer, dig out the first jar and see how much they've both grown in a year.

The Why + a Twist

The season jar turns invisible "time" into a visible row of jars — a child has no grip on "a year" or "the seasons turning," but four jars holding different things, lined up, and she gets it at a glance: "time is passing, things are changing." It's also the least-effort growth record there is. Twist: upgrade the jars to a "season corner" — keep a small patch of windowsill and each season swap in that season's nature bits, a picture, a line; everyone glances at it passing by and knows what season it is today.

Pick Just One This Week

Short on time? Do the "Season Jar." Collect a few things outside, drop them in an old jam jar, write a note, put the lid on — 20 minutes, zero skill, zero cleanup. But it's the longest-lived of the four: collect four jars in a year, line them up, and you've got the truest slice of your two daughters' year. Start one today; the other three can wait for the seasons to turn.

The process matters more than the result; being there matters more than teaching.
