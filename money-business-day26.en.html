Doing Together: Money & Little Business

2026-07-13 · Day 26
Money is something adults use every day but rarely sit down and talk about with kids. Today, no lectures — just coins and empty jars from around the house, and four money games: sort pocket money into "Save / Spend / Share" jars, open a family corner store to practice buying and making change, earn a first coin by doing extra chores, and finally run a real little stall to sell something. The point isn't to make your kid "love money" or "get good at making money" — it's to let her touch a few plain truths: money comes from work, once it's spent it's gone, and if you're selling something you have to figure out the cost first. The 3-year-old gets the sensory joy of coins clinking into a jar; the 9-year-old gets the brain work of counting, pricing, and deciding. Don't expect to teach personal finance in one evening. Pick the easiest one to start, and let money turn from an abstract number into something real in her hand — clinking, visible, and spendable.

1. Three Jars: Save, Spend, ShareSave / Spend / Share

⏱ 15 minMaterials: 3 empty jars + coinsEasiest start

Prep & Safety

Materials: three clear empty jars/bottles (jam jars, drink bottles — clear matters, so she can watch the money grow), sticky notes for labels, a handful of coins or small bills. How to play: label the jars "Save," "Spend," and "Share" — "Save" is for a bigger thing later, "Spend" is for small things she can buy this week anytime, "Share" is set aside to help someone or buy a gift. Every time she gets pocket money, split it into the three jars on the spot (say, 3 coins → one in each jar). The key is the clear jar: watching the "Save" jar slowly fill up, that "almost there" anticipation, beats any lecture a hundred times over.

Safety: Coins are the number-one choking/swallowing hazard for a 3-year-old — small, round, and irresistible to put in the mouth. An adult watches the whole time; once she's done, screw the lid on tight and put the jar out of her reach. Never leave her alone with loose coins. Coins are dirty — wash hands after. Glass jars break; give the little one a plastic bottle.

Age 3

Her job is the fun part — dropping coins in. Hand her a coin, she goes "clink" into the jar, one clink per coin, and she'll happily drop a whole handful. Don't expect her to understand "save/spend/share" — just let her play the sorting motion: you say "this one goes in the red jar," she does it. What she's building is the basic physical sense that "money comes one at a time, and once it's in, it stays in." Let her be in charge of screwing on lids and shaking the jar to hear it rattle.

Age 9

Her job is the decision — how should 3 coins split across the three jars? Evenly, or more into "Spend" if she wants something this week? Let her set the ratio and say why. Challenge her to set a goal for the "Save" jar: the thing she wants costs how much, and at this rate how many more weeks to get there? Have her work it out and write it on the label. Level up: each week, record "how much is in each jar," and after a month look at the change — the flow of money becomes visible for the first time.

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "little accountant," working out how to split each amount and helping little sister count; little sister is the "coin dropper," dropping the sorted coins into the right jars. Big sister says "this one goes in the Share jar," little sister drops it. Each can have her own set of three jars, but they manage them together and see whose "Save" jar fills first. Big sister thinks and allocates, little sister does the hands-on part — nobody steals the other's job.

The Why + Variation

The hardest thing about money to grasp is that it runs out — once spent, it's gone, and you have to earn more. The three jars turn that abstract fact into something you can see: an empty "Spend" jar means there's nothing left to spend, so if you want the bigger thing you have to hold back and feed the "Save" jar. This isn't teaching her to be stingy — it's her first taste of "choice": money is limited, and dropping it in this jar means it can't go in that one. Variation: give the "Share" jar a real purpose (enough saved to buy food for stray animals, or donate a bit) so sharing has a visible goal too.

2. The Family Corner StorePlay Store & Making Change

⏱ 20 minMaterials: household items + changeRole play

Prep & Safety

Materials: pick a few household items as "goods" (fruit, snacks, small toys, books), stick a handwritten price tag on each ($1, $3, $5 — keep numbers small); a handful of real coins and small bills as the "money"; a box for the register. How to play: one person is the "cashier," one is the "customer" who comes to buy with money. The customer picks items, adds up the total, pays; the cashier takes the money, makes change, says "thank you, come again." Play a few rounds, then swap roles. Real money beats play money — real coins and bills give the child a truer sense of "how much this is."

Safety: Coins again — when the 3-year-old is customer or cashier, that pile of loose coins and small bills on the table is a swallowing hazard. An adult sits right there, counts and collects everything when done, and lets not a single coin drop to the floor. Real money is dirty, especially coins — everyone washes hands after, and don't let her nibble the "goods" snacks mid-play. Don't hand the little one large real bills to play with — she'll tear them and it stings.

Age 3

She's best as the "customer" — pick something she wants, hand the money (the few coins you counted out for her) to the cashier, take the item, say "thank you." What she practices is the heart of a transaction: "if you want the thing you trade money for it, and once you hand the money over the thing is yours." Don't ask her to add up prices — just let her live the full loop of "pick → pay → get it." She also loves being the cashier who goes "beep" (a toy as a scanner), in charge of that one collecting-the-money moment.

Age 9

She's the "cashier," doing the real work: the customer bought a $3 item and a $5 item — total? Paid with $10 — how much change? Have her do the math in her head or lay out the coins; if she gets it wrong the customer (you) "objects," and she'll re-count with great seriousness. Level up: run a "buy two get one free" or "20% off" promotion so she has to figure the discounted price. Level up more: have her keep a running "sold this much today" ledger — addition and subtraction in a real setting beats worksheets.

Sisters Together

Big sister is the cashier (adding up, making change), little sister is the customer (picking goods, paying) — one handles the money, one spends it, a natural pair. Little sister carries her chosen items to the counter, big sister prices each one, totals it, makes change and hands over the goods; for the change part, big sister can have little sister help "count out three coins." Once they've got it, swap roles so big sister also experiences being the customer choosing what she wants most on a limited budget. Two players, one show, and both have real work.

The Why + Variation

Buying and selling looks simple, but it hides the lesson a child most needs: money and things are interchangeable, but once you trade it away you've given up something else. In making change, the 9-year-old is training number sense; in "trade money for a thing," the 3-year-old is grasping the idea of exchange — the origin point of all economic activity. Variation: give the customer a fixed budget (say only $10), forcing a choice among the things she wants — "not enough money, pick just one." That agony of choosing is the very first lesson in managing money.

3. Earn Your First CoinThe Value of Work

⏱ Flexible · a weekMaterials: paper + penValue of work

Prep & Safety

Materials: a sheet of paper for a "job price list," a pen, a few coins. How to play: together, agree on a few "extra" jobs (note: extra, not the things she should already do — tidying her own toys doesn't count), and put a price on each: wipe the table once = 50¢, help snap the vegetables = $1, water the plants for a week = $3… When she finishes a job, settle up on the spot or at the weekend, and the money she earned goes into the Activity 1 jars. The core: let her feel that "money is worked for, not wished into being." Keep amounts small — the point is the "work → pay" cause and effect, not how much money changes hands.

Safety: The jobs you assign must be age-appropriate — no knives, hot pans, high reaches, or heavy lifting for the 3-year-old; for the 9-year-old, jobs involving knives/hot water (cutting, carrying hot soup) need an adult watching or only the no-risk parts. Don't turn "earning" into pressure or a bargaining chip — "no pay, no work" is the wrong turn. Personal duties (brushing teeth, tidying her own toys) shouldn't be paid — those are just her responsibility; pay is only for "extra help to the household." Make that line clear to her.

Age 3

A 3-year-old doesn't grasp "earning," but she grasps "helping gets a reward." Give her a completely safe little job: put the snapped vegetable leaves in the basket, wipe the low table, match socks into pairs. When she's done, give her a coin for the jar and say "thank you for helping — you earned this." What she remembers is the happy loop: "I did a thing → got a clinking something → and got praised." Work and reward link up for the first time, which matters more than the money itself.

Age 9

She can play the real version: set the price list with you, even haggle over "how much for watering the plants a week" — that negotiation is itself a good lesson. Challenge her to work out "to save enough for the thing I want, how many times do I have to do this job," breaking a goal into individual pieces of work. Level up: let her try taking on "one big job at once" (tidy a whole bookshelf for $10), feeling that "a big job pays more but is also tiring" — her first weighing of "is it worth it, is it a good deal."

Sisters Together

Assign a job that "takes two to finish," with the pay split between them: put all the scattered books back together, water every plant on the balcony together. Big sister divides the work ("you carry, I place") and leads little sister; when done they split the pay evenly — and big sister may chew on "she's little, I did more, should I get more?" That fairness question is worth a chat. Little sister does the part she can, and the coin she earns is truly hers, not just handed over.

The Why + Variation

The illusion kids most easily hold is that "money just appears out of a grown-up's pocket / phone." Letting her work with her own hands and get paid shatters that instantly — behind the money is someone who spent time and effort. This isn't teaching her to be mercenary; it's giving her respect for "where Mom and Dad's money comes from." Variation: after a job, have her split the money into the three jars from Activity 1, so "earn → split → save → spend" forms a full circle — she's walked the whole life of a coin.

4. Run a Little StallCost & Pricing

⏱ Half a dayMaterials: homemade goods + a tableFinale · cost & price

Prep & Safety

Materials: something to sell (homemade lemonade/cookies, a yard sale of old toys and books, handmade cards), a table, a sign, a cash box with some change ready. How to play: this is today's graduation project, using all three earlier activities. First, figure the cost: how many lemons and how much sugar went into a cup of lemonade — per cup, that's how many cents? Then set the price: sell at what price to not lose money and still make some? Then set up at the neighborhood entrance, a flea market, or your doorstep (check with an adult first that the spot is allowed and safe), call out, sell, take money, make change. When it's over, settle up: revenue minus cost — a profit or a loss? Split the earnings into the three jars.

Safety: Running a stall must have an adult present the whole time, especially where strangers are involved — the child does not deal or take money from strangers alone, and an adult keeps the cash box. If selling food or drink, be hygienic: wash hands when making the lemonade/cookies, use clean containers, don't leave things out too long in the heat, and warn customers of allergens in the ingredients. Choose a safe spot (not roadside, not in traffic), and confirm with the property manager/market that stalls are allowed. Clean up completely when done — leave no trash.

Age 3

She's the "greeter" and "hander-over" — standing at the stall calling sweetly to passersby "want one?" (when she calls out, business always picks up), and when someone buys, she hands the item over. She can also drop the received coins into the cash box (an adult passes them to her — don't let her manage the money pile). What she experiences is the buzz and pride of "we're selling things, people are coming to buy, so exciting" — the accounts and prices are not her worry.

Age 9

She's the "little boss," leading the whole flow: figuring cost, setting price, taking money, making change, keeping accounts. Challenge her on the core lesson — pricing: too high and no one buys, too low and there's no profit, so how do you find the "people-buy-and-you-earn" price? Let her set it herself, and if after a while it's not selling, try lowering it. After packing up, walk her through the total: how much sold, how much cost, net profit, and average profit per cup? "Profit = revenue − cost" — she'll truly understand it after this one day.

Sisters Together

Big sister is the boss (pricing, taking money, accounting), little sister is the storefront (calling out, handing over goods, dropping coins) — turning the day into a real partnership business. Big sister does the brain work, little sister supplies the thing you most need: buzz — a little one calling out from the stall often works better than anything. When it's over they count the money together and split the profit into the three jars. "This is the first money we earned together" — that partnership pride is the best ending of the day, worth more than however much they made.

The Why + Variation

A stall compresses "running a business," a grown-up thing, into one day a child can touch: it turns out selling isn't just slapping on a price — you first spend money on materials (cost), the money you take has to earn the cost back, and only what's left is profit. The words "cost" and "profit," explained a hundred times, are no match for selling one cup of lemonade yourself. Variation: after the first sale, have her review "which sold best, was the price right, what to change next time" — that's the plainest "business sense," and the experience gained from doing it once is worth far more than the money made.

Just Pick One This Week

Got only fifteen minutes and want to start right now? Begin with 1. Three Jars — find three clear bottles, label them "Save / Spend / Share," and split the child's pocket money into them on the spot. Zero prep, and you can keep playing it daily (split once every time you give pocket money) — the easiest one to sustain long-term. If it's the weekend and the weather's good and you want a big, lively, skill-building project for the week, go for 4. Run a Little Stall: figure the cost, set the price, sell to real customers, pack up and do the accounts — walking through "earn → price → profit" in a single day. Two kids, one the boss and one calling out; when they've sold out they count the money and split the profit together, and that "we earned it!" thrill beats ten lectures on "money comes from work."

The process matters more than the result; being there matters more than teaching.
