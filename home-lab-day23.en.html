Doing Together: Home Lab

2026-07-10 · Day 23
Today the kitchen cupboard becomes a lab — baking soda, white vinegar, a lemon, some sugar, one egg, all already in the house. Four "chemistry surprises" that fizz, reveal hidden words, stack into layers, and turn rubbery: things you can see and touch, a hundred times better than a screen. One rule: you can play with all of it, but none of it goes in the mouth — tell both girls up front, "today is an experiment, not a snack." Failing is fine too — foam overflowing, colors muddling together, that's part of the fun. No need to do all four in one night; pick one free evening and take it slow.

1. Baking Soda VolcanoBaking Soda Volcano

⏱ 10 minMaterials: super simpleIt erupts · use a tray

Prep & Safety

Materials: 2 tbsp baking soda, a bit over half a cup of white vinegar, 1 drop dish soap, a few drops food coloring or juice, a small cup (the crater), and a big plate or tray underneath. Steps: baking soda into the cup → 1 drop dish soap → a few drops coloring → set the cup in the middle of the tray → pour the vinegar in all at once and step back to watch it "erupt."

Safety: vinegar stings eyes — keep faces back when pouring, and no rubbing eyes afterward. The foam and liquid are not for drinking or tasting. The finished foam is harmless — rinse it down the sink. Use the tray, or the table becomes a mess.

Age 3

She owns the key move — "pouring the vinegar": hand her the cup, steady her hand, and let her tip it into the crater — WHOOSH, out it comes, and she'll shriek with laughter. Let her pour the baking soda and coloring in earlier too; pouring things is her favorite job.

Age 9

She owns the "recipe" and the record-keeping: try more baking soda, or warm vinegar — does it erupt higher? Have her run it two or three times, changing one thing each round, and photograph to compare. Challenge her to answer: why does the dish soap make the foam whiter, thicker, and long-lasting?

Together

Big sister is the "volcano designer," loading the cup (baking soda, dish soap, color) and calling the countdown "three, two, one!"; little sister is the "igniter" who pours the vinegar on cue. One preps, one detonates — no fighting over jobs. Want another go? Reload and repeat, until the baking soda runs out.

The Why + a Twist

Baking soda is a "base," white vinegar is an "acid," and the moment they meet they react, making loads of carbon dioxide gas; the bubbles rushing out are the "eruption." The dish soap wraps that gas into dense foam, which is why it's white and lasts. Twist: swap in lemon juice for the vinegar — it erupts just the same, because lemon is acidic too.

2. Invisible InkInvisible Ink

⏱ 15 minMaterials: super simpleInvolves heat · adult step

Prep & Safety

Materials: half a lemon (squeezed, or bottled lemon juice), white paper, a cotton swab or fine brush, and a heat source (hair dryer on hot, or an adult with an iron / lamp bulb). Steps: squeeze lemon juice into a saucer → write or draw on the paper with the swab → let it dry (the writing vanishes completely) → apply heat and the brown letters slowly surface.

Safety: the heating step is done by an adult. Hot dryers, irons, and bulbs are all hot — keep the 3-year-old away from the heat source and never near a plugged-in iron. A hair dryer is safest — hold it back a bit and warm the paper slowly, safer than any flame. Lemon juice stings eyes, so wipe hands after writing.

Age 3

She writes the "secret note": scribble with lemon juice on the paper, then watch it disappear — "it's gone!" is her big moment. For the reveal she only watches from the side while you do it; let her count "one, two, three" as the letters appear, and keep her away from the heat.

Age 9

She designs a real "secret message": a code just for her little sister, dried and handed to you to develop. Challenge her with different "inks" — which shows up clearest: milk, apple juice, or sugar water? Have her guess: why do these turn brown when heated while the white paper doesn't?

Together

Play "pass the secret note": big sister writes the code in lemon juice, draws a treasure map, and hands the "blank paper" to little sister to "keep safe"; when the adult heats it to develop, little sister gets to shout "here it comes!" and be first to read the map and find the "treasure" (hide a candy or small toy ahead of time). Sister writes, sister seeks — the heating always stays in an adult's hands.

The Why + a Twist

Lemon juice has sugar and acid that soak into the paper fibers; when heated they "scorch" more easily than the paper, turning brown first, so the writing appears — the same idea as bread browning in the toaster. Twist: leave the note in strong sunlight by a window for a while and you can sometimes faintly make out the writing.

3. Density RainbowDensity Rainbow

⏱ 15 minMaterials: super simpleTakes patience

Prep & Safety

Materials: sugar, 4 cups, 4 colors of food coloring or juice, warm water, one tall glass, a spoon, and a dropper or small spoon. Make the sugar water: equal warm water in all 4 cups, then 1 spoon of sugar in cup 1, 2 in cup 2, 3 in cup 3, 4 in cup 4, stir each until dissolved, and color each differently. Stack the rainbow: pour the sweetest (4-spoon) into the bottom of the tall glass first, then slowly, over the back of a spoon, trickle the 3-spoon on top, then the 2-spoon, then the 1-spoon, layer by layer.

Safety: it's all sugar water and food coloring — harmless on the skin, but once colored it's best not to drink; toss it when done. Watch the 3-year-old with the glass, or use clear plastic cups to be safer.

Age 3

She owns "adding sugar" and "stirring": spoon sugar into the cups and stir hard until the sugar disappears. Stacking needs a steady hand, so leave that to big sister or you; she watches the colors appear layer by layer and points and counts, "one, two, three, four layers!"

Age 9

She owns the hardest part — the "stacking": learning to let the sugar water trickle gently down the spoon's back without disturbing the layer below. One slip and it mixes; this takes patience and touch, which is exactly her challenge. Have her think: why must the cup with the most sugar go at the very bottom?

Together

Assembly line: little sister is the "sugar mixer," adding sugar to the four cups, stirring, and coloring; big sister is the "layering engineer," taking the mixed water and carefully stacking it over the spoon's back. One supplies, one builds, together raising a rainbow tower. Collapses? Start over — even muddling it into one murky cup counts as a try.

The Why + a Twist

The more sugar, the "heavier" the water (denser); heavy sinks to the bottom, light floats on top, so the layers stay separated instead of mixing — like oil floating on water. Twist: once stacked, drop a single drop of plain water in with the dropper and watch where it "falls" and stops — it settles at the layer that's just as heavy as itself.

4. Bouncy Naked EggBouncy Naked Egg

⏱ 5 min prep + 2-day waitMaterials: super simpleWait · don't smash it

Prep & Safety

Materials: 1 raw egg (in its shell), a cup of white vinegar, and a cup deep enough to cover the egg. Steps: gently lower the egg into the cup → pour in vinegar to cover it → you'll immediately see tiny bubbles on the shell → cover with a cloth and leave for two days → the vinegar "eats" the shell away, leaving a soft, translucent membrane, and the egg becomes a "rubber egg" you can bounce a little.

Safety: it's a raw egg — wash hands after touching the egg or vinegar, and no touching the mouth (raw egg carries a salmonella risk). The finished rubber egg is not for eating and don't squeeze it hard — one break and it's all over your hand. Bounce it only from a very low height. It gets smelly while soaking, so keep it somewhere airy.

Age 3

She owns "the egg goes in" and the daily check-ins: gently place the egg in (you cradle her hand), then visit it with her each day — "is the shell gone yet?" The two-day wait is real anticipation for her. When it's done, let her poke the soft membrane with one finger to feel the "squish" — gently, no pressing.

Age 9

She owns the observation log: count the bubbles on the shell on day one, and note the shell thinning and vanishing over two days. When it's done she runs the "bounce test" — drop it gently from 2 cm, 5 cm, 10 cm — at what height does it break? Have her answer: where did the shell go? Why the bubbles?

Together

Big sister is the "recorder," taking a daily photo and describing how the shell has changed; little sister is the "guardian," reminding everyone "time to check the egg!" and counting bubbles. After two days, run the bounce test together: little sister drops it gently from low, big sister measures the height and calls stop. Nobody hogs the squeezing — the rubber egg is delicate.

The Why + a Twist

The shell is mostly calcium carbonate, and the vinegar's acid dissolves it bit by bit; the bubbles are the carbon dioxide given off by the reaction. With the shell gone, only that stretchy inner membrane holds the egg, which is why it can bounce. Twist: soak the finished rubber egg in plain water or sugar water for another day and it will swell or shrink as water moves in — that's osmosis, another magic trick you can watch.

Pick Just One This Week

No time but you want a "wow" moment? Do 1. Baking Soda Volcano — dig the baking soda and vinegar out of the cupboard, two minutes to set up, one pour and it erupts, both girls shrieking with delight, and cleanup is just a rinse in the sink. Want something easy that "grows" into a surprise? Set up 4. Bouncy Naked Egg: five minutes to drop it in a cup, leave the rest to time, and two days later it reveals itself — the waiting itself is the togetherness.

Process over outcome, presence over teaching.
