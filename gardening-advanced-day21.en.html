Doing Together: Gardening, Level Up

2026-07-08 · Day 21
After growing scallions and sprouting beans, this week goes one step deeper into the soil — not just watching things grow, but watching things cycle. How do fruit peels turn back into dirt? What is that earthworm actually doing down there? How does a flower become something you can eat, and who plays matchmaker? These four activities stretch "a pot of veggies" into a whole loop: scraps → soil → seed → flower → fruit → back to scraps. Nothing to buy — your balcony, flowerpots, and kitchen bin are the lab. Slow work, a week or two of waiting: perfect for growing a kid's patience. Pick one to start, and you'll have one more thing worth crouching down to look at each day.

1. A Compost Jar from Kitchen ScrapsTurn Trash into Soil

⏱ 15 min + wait 2–3 weeksMaterials: kitchen wastea little smelly

Prep & Safety

Materials: a clear big jar or lidded plastic box (poke a few air holes in the lid), today's fruit and veggie scraps (apple cores, banana peel, cabbage stalks — chop small so they break down faster), a handful of potting soil, a small handful of dry leaves or shredded newspaper, a spray bottle. How: layer it like a sandwich — scraps, soil, dry leaves — up to about two-thirds full → mist to "wrung-out-cloth" damp → put the lid on and set it somewhere shady. Every few days, give it a shake and add a little water if it's dry. In 2–3 weeks, open it up: the peels are turning dark and crumbly — becoming soil.

Safety: plant scraps only — no meat, fish, bones, oil, or cooked food (they stink and attract rats and flies). Don't seal it airtight — keep the air holes, or it goes foul. Keep it shady, not in the sun. Wash hands after handling scraps and soil; no rubbing eyes, no fingers in the mouth. A 3-year-old may want to eat the peels — set the rule "this is food for the bugs, not for us," and keep an eye out.

Age 3

Her job: "feeding" and "shaking." She drops the chopped scraps in one by one, grabs a handful of soil to cover them, then hugs the jar and shakes it (her favorite part). Every few days let her lean in and sniff — from "smells like fruit" to "smells like dirt," she'll notice the change with her nose.

Age 9

Her job: "ratios and management." She controls how thick each layer is, judges wet vs. dry (too wet = stink, too dry = no rot), and logs how far the peels have broken down. Challenge: run two jars side by side — one shaken and vented, one sealed shut. Compare after a few days: which composts well, which turns foul, and why? Then ask her: who exactly is "eating" the peels?

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "recipe boss" — decides what goes in and how many layers; little sister is the "hauler" — drops in scraps, adds soil, shakes the jar. They co-run a "dirt factory," open it once a week to inspect, and log "how far along today." When it's finally black soil, they dig it out together and spread it into a flowerpot — the dirt they made grows the next crop.

The Why + a Twist

Peels don't rot on their own — invisible bacteria and tiny bugs in the soil "eat" and break them into nutrients, turning them back into soil plants can use. Nature has no trash can; fallen leaves and peels all get recycled this way. Twist: drop a few earthworms in (next activity) and it composts faster — worm poop is the best fertilizer. Or bury a scrap of plastic next to a fruit peel and dig them up weeks later: the peel is gone, the plastic is still plastic — a one-glance lesson in "why use less plastic."

2. Meet the EarthwormsDig One Up, Make a Viewing Jar

⏱ 15 min to dig + watch a few daysMaterials: soil & a jar

Prep & Safety

Materials: a big glass jar or clear bottle, damp garden soil plus a little sand (layering lets you see the tunnels), a few fallen leaves or veggie scraps for the top, a few earthworms (dig in damp soil after rain, or under a flowerpot / in a planting strip), and a cloth or black paper to wrap the jar (worms fear light — wrapped up, they'll tunnel right against the glass). How: fill the jar to two-thirds with alternating layers of soil and sand → mist damp → add 2–3 worms and some leaves → wrap in black cloth, keep shady → each day, lift the cloth for a peek. Within 2–3 days you'll see winding tunnels.

Safety: a worm is a living creature — handle gently: don't squeeze, pull apart, or leave it in the sun (it dries out and dies). Watch for 2–3 days, then return it to the soil it came from — don't keep it long. Wash hands after touching soil and worms; no fingers in mouth or eyes. A 3-year-old grips without control and can crush a worm in one squeeze — hold her hand and cup it together gently, or let her just look, not grab.

Note: if there's a cut on a hand, put on a disposable glove before touching soil.

Age 3

Her job: "making the bed" and "feeding." She tears leaves and spreads them on top, mists a little water. At viewing time she lifts the black cloth and hunts for tunnels — "where did it dig to today?" One quick touch of the soft, cool worm (in your palm) is plenty — feeling a living thing move matters more than knowing its name.

Age 9

Her job: "care and record-keeping." Each day she sketches how the tunnels changed, judges if the soil needs misting, and notes how much of the leaves got eaten. Challenge: cover one half of the jar dark and leave the other half in light — watch which side the worm hides on, proving "worms avoid light." Then ponder: it has no eyes, so how does it know which side is bright? She's in charge of returning the worm safely to the soil afterward.

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "researcher" — records tunnels, manages the cloth and moisture; little sister is the "keeper" — feeds leaves, mists water. They co-manage one jar and crouch down together each day to find where the worm is. On release day they carry it back to the garden and say goodbye — after a few days of caring, a kid goes from "a bit scared" of this slippery little thing to "a bit sad to see it go."

The Why + a Twist

As a worm tunnels, it eats rotting leaves in the soil; the tunnels let air and water reach down, and its droppings are top-grade fertilizer — so worms are "free plows and free fertilizer crews." Lots of worms means healthy soil. Twist: merge the compost peels from Activity 1 with this jar to make a "worm compost bin," and see how much peel they clear in a few days. Or compare a jar with worms to one without: which stays loose, which packs hard?

3. Stake Out a Flower: Who's the Matchmaker?Watching Pollination

⏱ 20 min stakeoutMaterials: a flower + magnifier

Prep & Safety

Materials: somewhere with flowers (balcony plants, a flower bed, a park — flowering cucumber/squash/tomato are best, because you can watch "flower become fruit"), a magnifying glass, and optionally a cotton swab or soft brush. How: find an open flower, sit quietly beside it, and wait for bees, butterflies, and little flies — as they crawl in for nectar, yellow pollen sticks to their legs, and they carry it to the next flower. Use the magnifier to look at the pollen on the stamens and the "pollen pants" on a bug's legs. If no bugs show, be the bee yourself: dab pollen from one flower onto another's stamen with a swab.

Safety: bees can sting but rarely attack first — look, don't grab, don't swat, don't block its path; keep an arm's length and stay quiet, and it'll leave on its own. If anyone in the family is allergic to bee stings, take extra care — stay well back, or switch to hand-pollinating with a swab only. Don't shake the flower stem and spook the bugs. A 3-year-old reaches for bugs on instinct — hold her hand and agree "we're here to look, hands behind your back."

Age 3

Her job: "detective, spot the bug." Whoever sees a bee first whispers it (no shouting — that scares it off). Use the magnifier to peek at the yellow powder in the flower's heart. Let her be a bee — "buzzzz" — and pretend-dab from one flower to another with her finger, learning with her whole body that "pollen gets carried around."

Age 9

Her job: "observe and hand-pollinate." Count how many bugs come in a few minutes and what they are (bee / butterfly / hoverfly), and look closely at where the pollen sticks. Challenge: hand-pollinate a cucumber or squash flower (dab a male flower's pollen onto a female flower's stamen), mark it, and check in a few days whether that one sets a little fruit — she personally made a "flower become fruit." Then ask her: what if there were no bees at all?

Sisters Together

Big sister is the "recorder" — counts bugs, notes the time, spots where the pollen sits; little sister is the "lookout" — first to catch a visitor. If they hand-pollinate, big sister works the swab while little sister holds the magnifier on it. They guard one flower together and come back days later to see if "the one we helped" has a little fruit swelling.

The Why + a Twist

Many flowers must move pollen from one bloom to another to set fruit and seeds — but flowers can't walk, so they hire bees and butterflies as "delivery service": the bug comes for nectar and carries pollen off by accident. No pollinating bugs means far fewer fruits and vegetables for us. Twist: on one squash plant, leave two female flowers — hand-pollinate one, and bag the other in netting so no bug can reach it. Compare a few days later: the pollinated one fruits, the other wilts and drops — plain as day.

4. From Seed to Harvest: Pick What You GrewClose the Loop

⏱ 15 min to harvestMaterials: a pot of edibles

Prep & Safety

Materials: a pot of something grown and edible — the lettuce / bok choy / cilantro / cherry radish you planted before, or balcony mint or scallions; if you have nothing, buy a live potted vegetable with soil, "keep-growing" it a few days, then harvest. Plus clean scissors and a small basket or bowl. How: figure out which part is edible and when it's ready (leafy greens: pick the big outer leaves, leave the center to keep growing; radish: pull once the shoulder pokes above the soil; mint: snip the tips) → cut or pull → wash → use it in a dish or add it to a meal that same day. Let the loop — from a single seed to your mouth — close once, right in front of the kids.

Safety: scissors are for adults, or a 9-year-old under supervision — keep fingers clear of the blade, put them away right after, never hand over blade-first. A 3-year-old doesn't touch scissors — her job is pinching soft leaves or pulling a radish. Always wash the harvest before eating, and eat only the parts you know are edible (don't taste unfamiliar leaves). If a balcony plant was sprayed or is of unknown origin, don't eat it — just observe.

Age 3

Her job: "pick" and "wash." She pinches off tender leaves by hand, or grabs the radish tops and "heave-ho" pulls it out (the surprise moment!), then drops the veggies in a bowl of water to rinse. Picking a plant she watched grow and then eating it — that whole "I grew it, I picked it, I ate it" feeling matters far more than whether it tastes great.

Age 9

Her job: "judge and harvest." She figures out which leaves to take and how to cut so the plant keeps growing (pick outer, leave center), and leaves a few plants to set seed for the next crop. Challenge: count how many days from sowing to today and how much this pot yielded in total; then leave the strongest plant unpicked, let it flower and seed, extending "seed to harvest" into the next loop — "harvest to sow again."

Sisters Together

Big sister works the scissors, decides what to pick and what to leave for seed; little sister pinches soft leaves, pulls radishes, and carries the harvest to the sink. They harvest one pot together, carry the pickings into the kitchen, and add them to that day's meal. At the table, announce "this dish is one we grew ourselves" — even if it's just a few leaves, it tastes especially good.

The Why + a Twist

A seed, a little water and light, some time, plus a hand from the bugs — and it becomes a bite on your plate: that's the whole of farming, and there's a loop like this behind everything we eat. "Pick outer, leave center" lets leafy greens keep giving crop after crop, because the growing point at the center is left untouched. Twist: leave the strongest plant to bolt, flower, and set seed; collect and dry the seeds, then sow your "own seeds" next time — seed to harvest, then harvest back to seed, and the loop keeps turning.

Just Pick One This Week

No time, but want something to watch right away? Do the compost jar — don't toss today's fruit peels; chop them small, layer them with a handful of soil and shredded newspaper in a jar, mist, and lid it. Ten-odd minutes total, then just shake it every few days. It slowly turns dark and crumbly on its own, there's a new discovery every time the kids open it, and in the end you dig out real soil to grow the next pot — one activity that closes the whole "trash → soil → grow again" loop. Once that clicks, go dig up worms and stake out a flower.

Process over outcome, presence over teaching.
